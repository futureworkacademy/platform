## PART 2: WEEK 1 SITUATION BRIEFING

### "The Automation Imperative"
*Theme: Financing and communicating AI strategy*

You arrive at the office early on a crisp Monday morning, the Midwest sun barely peeking through the clouds. As you step into the bustling headquarters of Apex Manufacturing, the weight of your new role as CEO settles heavily on your shoulders. Just last week, the Board of Directors, led by the formidable Victoria Hartwell, delivered a stark ultimatum: modernize or be replaced. The stakes have never been higher.

Apex, a trusted name in the automotive parts supplier industry since 1987, is at a critical juncture. With annual revenues of $125 million and a workforce of 2,400 employees who have an average tenure of 7.2 years, the culture here is rooted in loyalty, craftsmanship, and a shared commitment to quality. Yet, the winds of change are blowing, and you must navigate them carefully.

In your first meeting, CFO David Chen presents alarming financial projections. He outlines how Apex's current 12% automation level is trailing far behind competitors like AutoTech Industries and PrecisionParts Co., which boast automation rates of 35-50%. Without significant investments in automation, Chen warns of inevitable margin erosion and declining market competitiveness. The urgency of the situation is palpable, especially as Thomas Richardson, your key customer from AutoCorp, has been increasingly vocal, demanding faster delivery times to meet their production schedule.

However, as you consider the implications of transformation, the atmosphere within the company grows tense. Rumors about potential job losses swirl among employees, igniting a sense of anxiety. As HR Director Sandra Williams emphasizes, maintaining morale and trust among your loyal workforce is paramount. The challenge lies in how to embrace technological advancement without alienating the very people who have contributed to Apex's success.

In the backdrop, you recall the cautionary tales of competitors. AutoTech's aggressive automation strategy resulted in strikes and a steep decline in customer satisfaction, while FastParts' neglect of workforce investment led to poor quality and ultimately made them a target for acquisition. In contrast, PrecisionParts has achieved a balance between automation and workforce empowerment, earning them the title of Best Employer. You realize that the path ahead will demand not just strategic financial decisions, but also a deep understanding of human dynamics.

### Key Developments

- **Board Ultimatum**: Victoria Hartwell sets a clear mandate for transformation, stating unequivocally that failure to modernize will lead to leadership changes.
- **Financial Insights**: CFO David Chen presents a stark analysis revealing that without automation, Apex will face diminishing profit margins and an inability to compete effectively.
- **Workforce Anxiety**: HR Director Sandra Williams reports a growing unease among employees, sparked by rumors of job cuts, which threatens the company's longstanding culture of loyalty.
- **Customer Pressure**: Thomas Richardson from AutoCorp expresses his frustration over delivery timelines, increasing pressure on Apex to accelerate its response to market demands.

### Stakeholder Pulse

- **Victoria Hartwell (Board Chair)**: Driven and focused, she feels a sense of urgency mixed with impatience. Her influence is unwavering, and she is determined to see Apex thrive in the face of automation.
- **David Chen (CFO)**: Analytical and pragmatic, Chen is worried about the financial health of the company. He feels a strong obligation to present the data clearly and persuade you to act swiftly.
- **Sandra Williams (HR Director)**: Concerned and empathetic, Williams is deeply aware of the emotional toll that rumors are taking on the workforce. She is committed to fostering open communication but feels the tension rising.
- **Frank Torres (Operations Manager)**: Caught between the demands of the board and the fears of his team, Torres feels conflicted. He respects the need for modernization but is apprehensive about how it will impact the skilled workforce he has nurtured.

### The Decision Ahead

As you contemplate the week ahead, you are faced with a series of critical decisions. Will you prioritize immediate automation investments at the risk of alienating employees? Or will you adopt a more measured approach, focusing on communication and workforce engagement while gradually implementing technological upgrades? Additionally, how will you address customer demands for faster delivery without sacrificing quality?

**Key Question**: How do you transform Apex Manufacturing with a loyal, long-tenured workforce while preserving the culture that has driven its success?

## PART 3: INTEL ARTICLES (Research Materials)

Students should read these articles before making their decision. In the platform, students who reference these materials in their essay receive higher scores.

### Article 1: WSJ Analysis - The Hidden Costs of Factory Automation

*Source: Wall Street Journal*

The allure of factory automation is undeniable, yet many companies overlook critical hidden costs:

**Integration Costs Beyond Equipment**: The initial capital investment is just the tip of the iceberg. Retrofitting facilities, enhancing infrastructure, and ensuring compatibility with legacy systems lead to unforeseen expenses.

**Training and Change Management**: Employees must be trained to operate new machines and understand underlying technologies. This is not a one-time expense - continuous education is essential. Companies that fail to invest in training face morale issues and increased turnover.

**Productivity Dip During Transition**: During implementation, productivity often dips as employees adapt. This temporary decline can be damaging in sectors with tight deadlines. The timeline for ROI can stretch significantly longer than anticipated.

**Maintenance and Upgrade Cycles**: Automation technologies require ongoing maintenance and regular upgrades. These costs are often underestimated. With rapid technological advances, companies may need to invest in new systems sooner than expected.

**Case Study - FastParts**: FastParts hastily transitioned to a fully automated production line without adequately assessing total costs. They faced extensive training requirements, a significant productivity dip, plummeting morale, and a wave of resignations. Within a few years, their technology became outdated, leading to quality issues that tarnished their reputation and made them an acquisition target.

**Source Code: AIM** (for citation in essays)

### Article 2: Harvard Business Review - Leading Through Technological Disruption

*Source: Harvard Business Review*

A framework for change leadership consists of five key elements:

1. **Vision**: Articulate a clear vision that resonates with employees at all levels
2. **Engagement**: Encourage open dialogue, solicit feedback, involve employees in the process
3. **Education**: Ensure employees possess skills to leverage AI effectively
4. **Resources**: Balance investments in technology with workforce development
5. **Metrics**: Establish clear KPIs to measure progress and enable course corrections

**Common CEO Mistakes During Transformation**:
- Underestimating the cultural impact of change
- Failure to communicate effectively, leading to skepticism
- Focusing too heavily on short-term gains at the expense of long-term viability

**Balance Between Urgency and Patience**: Pushing for immediate results leads to burnout. An overly cautious approach results in missed opportunities. A phased implementation strategy allows for experimentation and feedback.

**Building Coalition Among Skeptics**: Transform skeptics into advocates by actively listening to concerns and involving them in decision-making.

**Source Code: APX** (for citation in essays)

### Article 3: McKinsey Report - Manufacturing Automation Accelerates Post-Pandemic

*Source: McKinsey Global Institute*

**Key Statistics**:
- 70% of manufacturers have increased automation investments post-pandemic
- 50% plan to further expand automation within two years
- 50% increase in robotics and AI technology adoption since 2020
- 4 million workers left jobs in 2021; manufacturing saw 30% increase in unfilled positions

**ROI Timelines by Automation Category**:

| Category | Typical ROI Timeline | Examples |
|----------|---------------------|----------|
| Robotics | 12-18 months | Assembly line tasks |
| AI and Machine Learning | 18-36 months | Predictive maintenance |
| Process Automation (RPA) | 6-12 months | Data entry and reporting |
| Advanced Manufacturing (3D Printing) | 24-48 months | Prototyping and custom parts |

**Risk Factors**: High initial investment, integration complexity, workforce resistance.

**Early Adopters vs. Laggards**: PrecisionParts Co. (early adopter) maintained high employee morale while automating. AutoTech Industries (laggard) faced union strikes and declining customer satisfaction.

**Source Code: APX** (for citation in essays)

## PART 4: DECISION OPTIONS

Students must choose ONE of the three options below. Each has different financial and cultural implications.

### Option A: Conservative Path ($8M Investment)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Capital Investment | $8,000,000 |
| Expected Productivity Gain | 12% |
| Workforce Reduction | 85 positions over 18 months |
| Payback Period | 4.2 years |
| Net Present Value (5yr, 10% discount) | $2.1 million |
| Additional Debt Required | None |

**Summary**: A cautious, lower-risk approach that minimizes workforce disruption and maintains stakeholder relations. Aligns with current cash management. Slower competitive improvement.

---

### Option B: Phased Hybrid Approach ($12M Year 1, $8M Year 2)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Capital Investment | $20,000,000 total ($12M Y1 + $8M Y2) |
| Expected Productivity Gain | 28% by end of Year 2 |
| Workforce Reduction | 145 positions with 6-month retraining buffer |
| Payback Period | 3.1 years |
| Net Present Value (5yr, 10% discount) | $6.2 million |
| Retraining/Severance Costs | ~$2 million |

**Summary**: A balanced approach allowing iterative learning and adjustment. Provides competitive improvement while allowing time for workforce transition. Moderate risk.

---

### Option C: Aggressive Transformation ($18M Investment)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Capital Investment | $18,000,000 |
| Expected Productivity Gain | 35% |
| Workforce Reduction | 210 positions over 24 months |
| Payback Period | 2.8 years |
| Net Present Value (5yr, 10% discount) | $8.4 million |
| Additional Debt Required | $6 million at 7.2% APR |

**Summary**: Maximum financial return but highest cultural risk. Fastest competitive improvement. Raises concerns about employee morale, debt covenants, and union relations.

## PART 7: INSTRUCTOR NOTES

### Facilitating Class Discussion (Optional)

After submissions, consider discussing:

1. **Why did teams choose differently?** - Have students share their rationale in small groups
2. **The tension between financial and cultural health** - No option is "right" - each has trade-offs
3. **Stakeholder mapping** - Who benefits and who loses under each scenario?
4. **Real-world parallels** - Compare to actual manufacturing automation cases

### Key Takeaways for Students

- There is no single "correct" answer - the simulation rewards thoughtful analysis
- Financial performance and cultural health are equally weighted
- Referencing research materials demonstrates analytical rigor
- Stakeholder awareness is critical for effective leadership
- The decisions made in Week 1 cascade into subsequent weeks

In the full simulation, Week 1 decisions affect:
- Starting financial position (revenue, debt levels)
- Employee morale baseline
- Union sentiment
