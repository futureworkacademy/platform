# Institutional Partnership Proposal — Future Work Academy

**Confidential Proposal | Prepared for Academic Leadership**

## Executive Summary

Future Work Academy (FWA) is an AI-driven business simulation platform designed for graduate-level courses in Strategic Management, Business Analytics, Organizational Behavior, and related disciplines. Students assume executive roles at Apex Manufacturing, navigating 8 weeks of AI adoption challenges—managing workforce anxiety, stakeholder dynamics, and strategic trade-offs in a simulated environment that mirrors real-world complexity.

The platform addresses a significant gap in business education: while AI transformation is reshaping every industry, few MBA or graduate programs offer hands-on experiential simulations where students practice leading through this transition. FWA fills this gap with a scholar-practitioner approach grounded in established learning theory, aligned with AACSB standards for innovation in business education, and built with institutional compliance in mind from day one.

## Pedagogical & Theoretical Framework

FWA's design is grounded in eight established theoretical frameworks, each operationalized through specific platform features. For a more comprehensive treatment, see the published White Paper (futureworkacademy.com/white-paper) and Grading Methodology (futureworkacademy.com/methodology).

### 1. Experiential Learning Theory

**Kolb, D. A. (1984). Experiential Learning: Experience as the Source of Learning and Development. Prentice-Hall.**

FWA's 8-week simulation maps directly to Kolb's learning cycle: students encounter concrete experiences (weekly scenarios), engage in reflective observation (stakeholder analysis), develop abstract conceptualizations (strategic rationale), and apply active experimentation (decision implementation). Each week completes a full cycle, building cumulative learning.

### 2. Productive Failure

**Kapur, M. (2016). Examining productive failure, productive success, and constructive failure as learning designs. Instructional Science, 44(3), 289–306. DOI: 10.1080/10508406.2016.1166810**

Students encounter complex, ill-structured scenarios before receiving explicit instruction. Research demonstrates that this struggle—when properly scaffolded—produces deeper conceptual understanding and superior transfer than direct instruction alone. FWA's scenarios are intentionally designed so that no single "correct" answer exists, requiring students to navigate genuine ambiguity.

### 3. Formative Assessment

**Black, P. & Wiliam, D. (1998). Assessment and classroom learning. Assessment in Education: Principles, Policy & Practice, 5(1), 7–74. DOI: 10.1080/0969595980050102**

AI-powered rubric feedback after each weekly decision functions as formative assessment—continuous, criterion-referenced evaluation that enables students to improve over the simulation's duration. This contrasts with purely summative approaches, providing actionable insights while learning is still in progress.

### 4. Scaffolding

**Wood, D., Bruner, J. S., & Ross, G. (1976). The role of tutoring in problem solving. Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry, 17(2), 89–100. DOI: 10.1111/j.1469-7610.1976.tb00381.x**

FWA's 3-tier difficulty framework (Introductory / Standard / Advanced) operationalizes scaffolding by providing adjustable support structures. Introductory scenarios offer more guidance and clearer stakeholder signals; Advanced scenarios introduce greater ambiguity, conflicting data, and higher-stakes trade-offs—matching Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development.

### 5. Feed-Forward Feedback

**Hattie, J. & Timperley, H. (2007). The power of feedback. Review of Educational Research, 77(1), 81–112. DOI: 10.3102/003465430298487**

Grading reports include not just scores but actionable improvement guidance—specific recommendations for strengthening evidence quality, stakeholder analysis, and risk mitigation. This feed-forward orientation ensures feedback drives future performance, not just retrospective judgment.

### 6. Situated Cognition

**Brown, J. S., Collins, A., & Duguid, P. (1989). Situated cognition and the culture of learning. Educational Researcher, 18(1), 32–42. DOI: 10.3102/0013189X018001032**

Learning is embedded in a realistic business context—Apex Manufacturing—with authentic organizational dynamics, competing stakeholder interests, and genuine uncertainty. Students don't study AI transformation abstractly; they practice it in a simulated environment that mirrors real-world complexity.

### 7. Stakeholder Salience

**Mitchell, R. K., Agle, B. R., & Wood, D. J. (1997). Toward a theory of stakeholder identification and salience. Academy of Management Review, 22(4), 853–886. DOI: 10.5465/amr.1997.9711022105**

17 character profiles with quantifiable traits (influence, hostility, flexibility, risk tolerance) model real organizational complexity. Students must navigate competing interests—from resistant union representatives to enthusiastic early adopters—developing stakeholder management skills grounded in established theory.

### 8. Single-Iteration Critique

**Kayes, D. C. (2002). Experiential learning and its critics: Preserving the role of experience in management learning and education. Academy of Management Learning & Education, 1(2), 137–149. DOI: 10.5465/amle.2002.8509336**

FWA's compressed 8-week timeline forces decisive action under uncertainty—reflecting real managerial conditions where leaders rarely get to "replay" decisions. This design choice addresses critiques that experiential simulations often allow unrealistic do-overs, instead building comfort with irreversibility.

## Learning Outcomes & Assessment Design

### Structured Response Format

Each weekly decision requires a structured 3-part response:

1. **Evidence-Based Rationale** — Students must cite specific data from intel articles, stakeholder profiles, and scenario briefings to justify their strategic choice
2. **Stakeholder Trade-off Analysis** — Identification of which stakeholders benefit and which bear costs, with explicit reasoning about competing interests
3. **Risk Mitigation Planning** — Anticipation of potential failure modes and contingency strategies for their chosen approach

### 4-Criterion Rubric

All responses are evaluated against four consistently defined criteria:

- **Evidence Quality (25 pts)** — Specific citations from provided research, data, and scenario materials
- **Reasoning Coherence (25 pts)** — Logical flow connecting evidence to conclusions with clear causal reasoning
- **Trade-off Analysis (25 pts)** — Balanced consideration of competing stakeholder interests and organizational tensions
- **Stakeholder Consideration (25 pts)** — Depth of engagement with affected parties and downstream consequences

### Evidence-Based Rationale

The automated assessment approach is grounded in established research on automated essay evaluation (Shermis & Burstein, 2013). FWA's implementation emphasizes criterion-referenced evaluation with published rubrics, distinguishing it from opaque "black box" scoring systems.

### Radical Transparency

All rubric criteria, scoring bands (Excellent ≥93%, Good ≥72%, Adequate ≥52%, Poor <52%), and evaluation standards are published and visible to students before, during, and after submission. Optional curved scoring using Z-score normalization is available for instructors who prefer relative grading, but is disabled by default to prioritize criterion-referenced feedback.

## AI Ethics & Transparency

FWA uses AI across three domains: essay evaluation, content generation, and media production. Each domain carries distinct ethical considerations. Our approach prioritizes explainability (Liao et al., 2020), demographic neutrality (Baker & Hawn, 2022), and full disclosure of AI involvement.

### Scoring Bias Safeguards

Algorithmic bias in educational AI is a documented concern (Mehrabi et al., 2021; Baker & Hawn, 2022). FWA implements multiple layers of protection:

- **Blind Evaluation** — No student name, email, gender, age, ethnicity, or institutional identifier is ever sent to the AI. The evaluator receives only the response text and simulation context.
- **Deterministic Configuration** — AI models are configured with low-temperature settings to minimize output variance, producing highly consistent scores for the same response across evaluations.
- **Source-Based Rubrics** — Scoring rewards specific data citations and logical reasoning—not writing style, vocabulary sophistication, or rhetorical flourish. This reduces sociolinguistic bias.
- **Explicit Calibration** — The AI prompt includes scoring calibration guidance that prevents systematic score compression, ensuring strong work is rewarded generously rather than clustered around a safe middle.
- **Human Override** — Instructors can review and adjust any AI-generated score. The system is designed as an evaluation assistant, not a replacement for instructor judgment.
- **Distribution Monitoring** — The Week Summary Dashboard provides visual score distribution charts per week, enabling instructors to identify and investigate scoring anomalies across cohorts.

### AI-Generated Media Disclosure

FWA uses AI to generate visual and audio assets for the simulation. In the interest of full transparency:

| Asset Type | Technology | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Character Headshots | OpenAI DALL-E (gpt-image-1) | 17 stakeholder character portraits are AI-generated using detailed prompts specifying professional corporate photography style. No real individuals are depicted. |
| Advisor Audio | ElevenLabs Text-to-Speech | Phone-a-Friend advisor audio clips are synthesized from text scripts using voice profiles designed for each character persona. All advisors are fictional. |
| Hero & Feature Images | OpenAI DALL-E | Background images on public-facing pages (boardroom scenes, classroom settings) are AI-generated for editorial illustration purposes. |
| Brand Assets | AI-Assisted Design | Logo lockups and brand iconography were developed with AI design assistance and refined through manual iteration. |

Full AI transparency documentation—including exact prompts, model configurations, and data handling policies—is maintained internally and available for institutional review upon request.

### AI Data Handling & Student Privacy

Student essay submissions are evaluated by OpenAI's GPT-4o-mini via API endpoints governed by OpenAI's enterprise data usage policies. The following safeguards are in place:

- **No PII Transmission** — Only the response text and simulation context (scenario description, available data sources, rubric criteria) are sent to the AI. Student names, emails, and identifiers are never included.
- **No Model Training** — Under OpenAI's data usage policy for API customers, inputs and outputs are not used to train or improve models. Data retention is governed by the provider's enterprise terms, with zero-day retention available upon request.
- **Explainable Outputs** — Every AI evaluation returns structured feedback (per-criterion scores, specific strengths, improvement recommendations)—not just a number. Students can see exactly why they received their score (Liao et al., 2020).
- **Radical Transparency** — The complete rubric, scoring bands, and evaluation criteria are published and visible to students while writing—before, during, and after submission. There are no hidden formulas or opaque algorithms.

### Ongoing Validity & Fairness Monitoring

AI assessment is not a "set and forget" system. FWA provides built-in tools for continuous validation:

- **Score Distribution Dashboard** — Visual histograms and distribution charts per week allow instructors to spot skewed distributions, ceiling/floor effects, or scoring drift across the simulation timeline.
- **Instructor Override Tracking** — When instructors adjust AI scores, the system logs the original and modified values—creating a dataset for inter-rater reliability analysis between AI and human evaluators.
- **Student Feedback Surveys** — Per-week surveys capture student perceptions of fairness, difficulty, and learning value on validated scales—including self-efficacy (Bandura, 1997) and productive struggle (Kapur, 2016) constructs—providing triangulated data on assessment quality.
- **Difficulty-Adjusted Evaluation** — The 3-tier framework adjusts AI evaluation expectations by difficulty level—Introductory scenarios award partial credit more generously, while Advanced scenarios maintain rigorous professional standards—ensuring fairness across heterogeneous student populations.

We welcome institutional partnerships that include formal validity studies—comparing AI-generated scores with instructor-assigned scores across demographic groups—as a publishable research outcome.

### Accessibility Posture

FWA is committed to ensuring equitable access for all learners, including those with disabilities. The platform's UI is built on Radix UI primitives—a component library specifically engineered for accessibility—providing a strong architectural foundation.

**Architectural Accessibility Features:**

- **Keyboard Navigation** — Core UI components (menus, modals, tabs, forms) are built on Radix primitives designed for keyboard operability with visible focus indicators. Custom interactive widgets will undergo manual keyboard testing before pilot deployment.
- **ARIA Semantics** — Screen reader support via ARIA labels, roles, and live regions is provided through Radix-based components. Full conformance across all views is pending formal WCAG audit.
- **Alt Text on Images** — Development standards require descriptive alt text on meaningful images and empty alt attributes on decorative images, following WCAG guidance.
- **Focus Management** — Radix-based modals trap focus correctly, dialogs return focus on close, and tab order follows logical reading sequence.
- **Light & Dark Themes** — Full theme support with defined foreground/background contrast ratios for both modes, reducing visual fatigue.
- **Semantic HTML** — Heading hierarchy, landmark regions, and form labels are used to convey content structure to assistive technologies.

**Known Limitations & Roadmap:**

- **WCAG 2.1 AA Formal Audit** (Planned, Q2 2026) — A professional external accessibility audit is scheduled. Following audit completion and remediation, FWA will publish a Voluntary Product Accessibility Template (VPAT/ACR)—the standard conformance report universities request during procurement.
- **Color Contrast Verification** (Planned) — While the platform uses defined contrast ratios in both themes, a systematic audit against WCAG 2.1 AA contrast minimums (4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text) has not yet been completed.
- **Color Blindness Review** (Planned) — Status indicators currently use green/amber/red color coding. A review is planned to ensure all color-coded information is also conveyed through icons, labels, or patterns—so no information is communicated by color alone.
- **Custom Widget Keyboard Testing** (Planned) — While Radix-based components provide built-in keyboard support, custom interactive elements (audio players, specialized controls) require manual keyboard and screen reader testing—scheduled as part of the WCAG audit cycle.
- **Multimedia Transcripts** (Available) — All advisor audio content includes full text transcripts, ensuring audio-dependent content is accessible to deaf and hard-of-hearing users.

FWA's accessibility approach mirrors its compliance philosophy: build the right architecture first, then validate with formal audits. We welcome accessibility feedback from institutional partners and are committed to addressing identified barriers before pilot deployment.

## Understanding FERPA

FERPA (Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act) is a federal law—not a certification or compliance badge that vendors can "achieve." It governs how educational institutions handle student records. Software vendors like FWA support institutional FERPA compliance through:

- **Data Processing Agreements (DPAs)** that contractually bind the vendor to institutional data handling requirements
- **Security assessments** (HECVAT, SOC 2) that demonstrate technical safeguards
- **Architectural decisions** that minimize data exposure

FWA's Privacy Mode is specifically designed to reduce FERPA complexity: when enabled, students enroll with pseudonymous identifiers—no real names, no email addresses, no institutional IDs are stored in the platform. This means there is significantly less "education record" data to protect in the first place.
